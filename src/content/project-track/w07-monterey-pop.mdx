Ravi Shankar performed at the Monterey International Pop Festival in June 1967 — the same festival that featured Jimi Hendrix, Janis Joplin, and Otis Redding, among others. The audience had come for rock music. What they received was Indian classical music performed at the highest level by one of its greatest practitioners, with his tabla partner Alla Rakha.

Watch what happens to the audience.

## Two Axes

Indian classical music operates on two axes simultaneously that Western music largely collapses into one.

**Raga** is the melodic framework — not a scale but a set of notes with rules about how you approach and leave each one, which ones you emphasize, what time of day the raga belongs to, what emotional state (*rasa*) it cultivates. *Raga Bhairav* — which uses a flattened second and sixth — sounds simultaneously ancient and searingly emotional to Western ears. This is a candidate for your Indian intrusion moment.

**Tala** is the rhythmic cycle — a repeating pattern of beats grouped in specific ways that the soloist and tabla player navigate together. *Rupak tala* is 7 beats (3+2+2). *Jhaptal* is 10 beats (2+3+2+3). These are not exotic time signatures that exist to be interesting — they are containers for long improvisation, structures that hold tension and generate resolution.

## The Sam

The end of each tala cycle is called the **sam** — the "one" — and when soloist and drummer arrive at it together after elaborate improvisation, the resolution is equivalent to your kintsugi moment. Not the wound, but the repair. The completion after the elaboration. The earned return.

In your piece, the sam principle gives you a structural resolution device that is more sophisticated than the standard Western cadence. It is not just "arriving home" — it is arriving home *exactly on schedule after deliberate absence*, and the precision of the arrival is what makes it feel like fate.

## The Audience Transformation Is Your African Drumming Character

Watch the Monterey footage. The audience came not knowing what they were about to hear. By the end of the performance, they are somewhere else. Not where they expected to be when they sat down. The music moved them through a series of emotional and perceptual states — curiosity, confusion, engagement, recognition, surrender — without any of the tools they were expecting.

That transformation is the effect your African Drumming character has on the other characters in *Jus Pulsus*. Not the force of the drums but the recognition — something older than any of them arriving in the room and reorganizing the room's priorities. Shock. Curiosity. The thing that feels like memory even though they have never heard it before.

Shankar is showing you how to do that.
