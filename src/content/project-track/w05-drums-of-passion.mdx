Olatunji was a Nigerian Yoruba drummer who arrived at Columbia University on a scholarship in the 1950s. He recorded this album as a demonstration — addressed to Western academia, which had categorized African polyrhythmic drumming as primitive noise — that the tradition constituted a complete and sophisticated musical system, one that had been developing for millennia before the Western tradition existed.

Columbia Records released it. It sold over five million copies. It introduced a civilization to what rhythm actually is.

## What Polyrhythm Does That Nothing Else Can

There are no chord changes in the Western sense. There is no melody in the Western sense. There are multiple rhythmic cycles of different lengths running simultaneously — a cycle of four beats against a cycle of three, a cycle of seven against both — creating emergent patterns at their intersections that no single drummer is playing. The complexity arises from the combination of simple things.

This is your cosmogony in miniature. The universe does not begin with a plan — it begins with simple elements that generate incomprehensible complexity through their interaction. The music demonstrates the mechanism.

## The Akiwowo Threshold

The track *Akiwowo* is where most Western listeners first feel their rhythmic framework dissolve. The cycles are long enough and the combinations intricate enough that your pattern-recognition system cannot find the ground. Stay with that disorientation. Do not reach for it. Let the ground go.

That dissolution is the lesson. The lesson is not academic — it is physical. Your body is having its assumptions restructured in real time. This is what the deaf raver knows that you have been theorizing about.

## Why Your Body Responds Before Your Mind

Human bodies respond to this music before any conscious processing occurs. This is not a metaphor or an aesthetic claim. Rhythm precedes language in evolutionary development. It precedes pitch. It is the oldest musical technology on earth, and some part of your nervous system recognizes it as something that was there before the newer parts arrived.

This is why the African Drumming character has gravitational power over every other character in *Jus Pulsus*. Not because it is louder or more insistent — but because it is *older*. It speaks to a layer of the nervous system that predates everything else you know about music.

## The Character It Is Teaching You About

The African Drumming character in your piece is the character that makes the other characters forget what they were doing. Not through force — through recognition. It is grandmother's cooking arriving in a room where people are arguing about something they no longer care about as much as they thought.

Olatunji is the primary reference for this character's gravitational pull. Listen to how *you* respond. That is the material.
