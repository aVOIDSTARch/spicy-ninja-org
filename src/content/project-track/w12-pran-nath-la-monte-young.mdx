These are two works that both treat the drone — a single sustained pitch or narrow pitch cluster — as a complete cosmological statement.

## Pran Nath: Emergence from Almost Nothing

Pran Nath was a kirana gharana vocalist — the kirana style emphasizing extremely slow, meditative unfolding of the raga, with the tambura drone as the foundation everything grows from. Listening to Pran Nath is listening to something emerge from almost nothing over long time spans. A single note, held, ornamented, approached from different angles over extended duration.

This is how your Cosmos awakens. The double bass's geological slowness. The viola's searching above a sustained foundation. The Cosmos is not announcing itself — it is *coming into presence* from the ground up, the way a continent becomes visible through fog.

## La Monte Young: The Mathematics of Tuning

La Monte Young is the godfather of minimalism — his drones influenced Philip Glass, Terry Riley, and Pran Nath himself. *The Second Dream* is a piece for four trumpets playing specific pitches from the harmonic series — held, sustained, barely moving — for as long as the performers can sustain it.

The harmonics between the pitches produce interference patterns (beating) that shift as the performers' tones drift microscopically. The piece is alive in the mathematics of the tuning. Nothing is composed in the conventional sense — the mathematical relationships between the pitches compose themselves as the performers hold them.

## What Pulsus' Presence Sounds Like

Before Pulsus asserts a rhythmic grid, before the BPM arrives, Pulsus *exists* as a drone — a harmonic presence in the void alongside the bass and bell. This is what that presence sounds like. Not rhythm. Not melody. A sustained tone whose overtone series is audible. The pressure from inside before it becomes a beat.
