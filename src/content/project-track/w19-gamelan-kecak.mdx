Balinese gamelan is built on **interlocking** (*kotekan*) — a technique where two players divide a single melodic line between them. Neither player plays the full melody. Player A plays the odd beats; Player B plays the even beats. Together they produce a melody faster than either could play alone. The combined melody is not composed — it is the mathematical product of coordination.

## This Is Your Act III

In Act III, when all your orbital pairs are simultaneously present, what the listener hears is not any single character — it is the emergent pattern produced by their simultaneous presence. This is kotekan at cosmological scale. Your characters are the players. The music nobody composed is the ecstatic tempest.

## The Kecak Mathematics

Kecak takes this further: 50–150 men chanting "cak-cak-cak" in interlocking rhythmic patterns. If voice group A claps on beats 1, 5, 9 of a 12-beat cycle, group B on beats 2, 6, 10, group C on 3, 7, 11, group D on 4, 8, 12 — the four groups together produce continuous 16th notes at four times any individual's speed.

This is combinatorial emergence: N groups each doing 1/N of the work produce 1× the full result.

## Why It's Dangerous

This also explains why the ecstatic tempest is the crisis of the piece. At full participation — all characters simultaneously present and interlocking — the emergent pattern is maximally complex and maximally overwhelming. This is what Pulsus fears. This is why the look happens when it happens. At full combinatorial density, the music risks exceeding its container. The kintsugi wound is not the cause of the crisis. The full presence of all characters is the crisis. The wound is the cost of what was required to end it.
