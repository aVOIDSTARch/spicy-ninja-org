Wendy Carlos took Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier and Brandenburg Concertos and performed them entirely on a Moog synthesizer in 1968. It proved that electronic music could carry emotional and structural complexity equal to the greatest acoustic music ever written. It also sold over a million copies.

## The Recording Process as Revelation

Carlos was forced to perform Bach's counterpoint — multiple independent voices in dialogue — one voice at a time, then layer them by overdubbing. In doing so, she made the architecture visible in a way that is harder to hear in a live performance. You can hear each voice independently before hearing them together.

This is your compositional method. You are building your characters one live_loop at a time, then layering them. Carlos shows you what that practice looks like when applied to the most sophisticated contrapuntal music ever written — and how much the individual lines can stand alone before they meet.

## The Brandenburg Third

The Brandenburg Concerto No. 3 is particularly instructive: three groups of three instruments in conversation. Your three orbital pair layers in Act III. Not three solo voices but three *groups*, each internally coherent, in dialogue with the other two groups. Study how Bach maintains the independence of all three groups while creating a unified forward momentum.

## You Are Writing Fugues

You may not call them fugues. They are fugues. Each live_loop is a voice. Your characters are in counterpoint. The orbital mechanics are phase relationships between voices. The ecstatic tempest is stretto — voices overlapping before the previous voice completes. Carlos shows you what your piece looks like from the Bach end.
