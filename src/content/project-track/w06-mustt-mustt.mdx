Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan was a Pakistani qawwali master — qawwali being the devotional music of Sufi Islam, designed as a technology for achieving *hal*: the state of ecstatic spiritual dissolution that the whirling dervishes achieve through spinning. He is widely considered the greatest vocalist of the twentieth century by those who have listened seriously. His improvisational range was physiologically anomalous — he could sustain passages and ornamentation that other trained vocalists confirm should not be physically possible.

## Two Entry Points

*Mustt Mustt* (1990) is the accessible entry point — a studio collaboration with Canadian guitarist and producer Michael Brook that bridges qawwali and Western ears without compromising either. The title phrase is a Punjabi word for a state of intoxication that is also a state of devotion — being so consumed by love for the divine that ordinary consciousness dissolves. Start here if the Paris concert feels impenetrable.

The Paris concert (1988) is the real architecture. A live qawwali performance that builds over forty-five minutes from a single repeated phrase into something that sounds like the air itself is vibrating with accumulated charge. Watch the audience. They came expecting a concert and they are inside a ritual by the end.

## The Capacitor Mechanism

The repetition is not laziness. It is the mechanism. The same phrase repeated accumulates emotional charge the way a capacitor accumulates electrical charge. Each repetition adds to the total without releasing it. The audience accumulates alongside the charge. When the release comes, it releases everything simultaneously — every repetition, every instance of withheld energy, all at once.

This is the ecstatic tempest from the inside. This is the mechanism for the section of *Jus Pulsus* that corresponds to the dervish sequence — the whirling that is not mania but precision, not loss of self but a different kind of self that exists only inside the motion.

## The Ensemble as Your Three Fates

Watch his backing vocalists. They are not accompaniment — they are a narrative function. They respond to his improvisations with confirmation, challenge, echo, amplification. When he takes a phrase somewhere unexpected, they witness it and reflect it back. When he arrives somewhere, they confirm the arrival.

This is the function of your Three Fates in *Jus Pulsus*. They do not tell the story — they witness it and make the witnessing audible. Nusrat's ensemble shows you exactly how that works in practice, in real time, over the arc of a long performance.

## The Deaf Raver Validated

This work is on the syllabus because it validates something you already know from the inside of a rave at 4am. Music is a physical technology for altered states. This is not metaphorical. Sufi practice formalized it, developed it, refined it over centuries. The structural mechanism that Nusrat is deploying — accumulated repetition, sustained duration, call and response, deliberate withholding of release — is the same mechanism that a great DJ deploys.

The difference is cosmology, not technique.
