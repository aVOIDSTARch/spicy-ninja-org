*Chants of India* was produced by George Harrison — who had studied sitar with Shankar beginning in 1966 and maintained a close friendship and musical collaboration until Shankar's death in 2012. Harrison brought his understanding of Western production to Shankar's material without distorting it. The result is an album of vedic chants and classical pieces that is both deeply traditional and unusually accessible to Western ears.

This is the companion to the Monterey recording: where Monterey showed you the improvisational vitality of Indian classical music, *Chants of India* shows you its contemplative, devotional dimension — the other pole.

## Raga Bhairav

The raga *Bhairav* is heard in several pieces on this album. Its defining feature is a flattened second and sixth — a scale degree that sits a half-step above the root, creating a characteristic interval that Western ears associate simultaneously with great antiquity and with a specific kind of emotional rawness.

This raga is a candidate for your Indian intrusion moment in *Jus Pulsus* — the moment when the Indian musical system breaks through the surface of the piece, when the Rhythm Antagonist asserts its full identity. The flattened second creates an immediate and visceral emotional response even for listeners who have never encountered it before. It sounds like something very old insisting on being heard.

## Rasa as Design Parameter

In Indian classical theory, *rasa* is the emotional essence that a piece of music is designed to produce. There are traditionally nine rasas: love, humor, pathos, fury, heroism, terror, disgust, wonder, and tranquility. A composer or performer does not aim for emotional impact as a general goal — they aim for a specific *rasa*, and they choose formal elements (raga, tala, tempo, ornament) that are traditionally associated with producing that particular state.

This is the most important conceptual tool in this module. You can work this way. You can identify the emotional state you want to produce in the listener at a specific moment in *Jus Pulsus* — not "emotional" or "intense" or "sad" but a specific, named state — and then choose the formal elements that have been shown, across centuries of practice, to produce that state.

Design the state. Choose the tools. This is not manipulation. This is craft.

## The Yun Principle

Pay close attention to the space between notes in the more contemplative passages — particularly the bell-like tones in the opening tracks. The silence between strikes carries weight. The space is not empty — it is the space that the preceding tone has charged, and its duration is a compositional parameter as deliberate as the duration of the tone itself.

In *Jus Pulsus*, your bell character operates on this principle. The bell does not strike continuously. When it sounds, it charges the silence that follows. The silence lasts exactly as long as it needs to — not long enough to release the tension, not short enough to prevent its accumulation. The yun principle governs your bell's relationship with time.
