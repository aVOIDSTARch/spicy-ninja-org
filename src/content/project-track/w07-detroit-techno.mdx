Detroit techno was invented by three Black men — Juan Atkins, Derrick May, Kevin Saunderson — in Detroit in the mid-1980s. They were working-class kids from a city the American economy had abandoned, in love with Kraftwerk's European machine music and George Clinton's funk, and they synthesized something that had never existed: rhythm as the primary musical subject in electronic music.

## Strings of Life

"Strings of Life" is the most important track in this module. It begins with a piano riff over a four-on-the-floor kick drum. Then the strings come in — and the room changes. Derrick May described what he was trying to do as "George Clinton and Kraftwerk stuck in an elevator."

That description contains your entire compositional method: two complete musical universes pressed together until something new emerges that is neither of them. This is how your African Drumming and Western harmonic characters interact. Not fusion — collision that produces a third thing.

## Underground Resistance

Underground Resistance is the political, harder, more radical branch — anonymous Detroit producers working under total opacity because they wanted the music to be primary. Their aesthetic is your aesthetic: bespoke, eccentric, uncompromising, concerned with questions larger than the genre. The music stands without biography, without image, without explanation. It either works or it doesn't, and they decided it worked.

## Your Direct Lineage

The trance and progressive electronic music you grew up inside is directly downstream of Detroit. Van Dyk, Oakenfold, Sasha — all tracing back through May and Atkins. Hearing "Strings of Life" is seeing where your musical DNA entered the pool. The orbital mechanics of *Jus Pulsus* — layered rhythmic cycles building toward an ecstatic release — are built on rhythmic architecture this music established in the 1980s and has been developing ever since.
