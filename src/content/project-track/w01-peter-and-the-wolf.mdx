Prokofiev wrote this in four days for a children's theater in Moscow with a single pedagogical brief: introduce children to orchestral instruments. Do not let the children's label deceive you. He produced one of the most sophisticated programmatic structures in the repertoire — a complete lesson in how music creates characters, differentiates them, and then destroys one of them in a way that leaves a permanent mark on everything that follows.

## The Instrument Casting

Each character gets an instrument and a leitmotif. The duck is an oboe. The wolf is three French horns in unison. The grandfather is a bassoon with a specific grumbling rhythmic pattern. Peter is the strings. The bird is a flute, quick and high. The cat is a clarinet — smooth, deliberate, slightly sinister.

These are not arbitrary assignments. Prokofiev matched timbral personality to narrative personality with surgical precision. The wolf's horns are imposing because horns are physically large instruments whose overtone series is naturally dominant and authoritative — they fill a room the way a predator fills a doorway. The duck's oboe is reedy, slightly nasal, slightly comic — because that is what an oboe *is* when you strip away reverence for it.

This is the method *Jus Pulsus* inherits directly. When you cast the French horn as Pulsus's primary voice, you are doing exactly what Prokofiev did: choosing the instrument whose physical character *is* the character. The oboe returns in your piece as the voice of wisdom precisely because Prokofiev established that the oboe is the instrument that knows more than it lets on.

## The Kintsugi Moment

Listen for the moment near the end when the duck's theme appears *inside* the wolf's theme after the wolf has swallowed her. The duck is gone. Her music survives — not intact, not destroyed, but transformed by what happened to it. It lives now inside the thing that consumed her, audible if you know to listen for it.

This is the kintsugi. The character is consumed. The musical identity persists as a scar inside the new whole. The wolf carries her now. He is not the same wolf he was before the swallowing.

This is the structural and philosophical mechanism your piece inherits. When Eurydice is lost, her material does not disappear — it continues inside Pulsus, changed, audible to those who know where to listen.

## Simultaneous Themes

Several times in the piece, two characters' themes play at the same time. This sounds simple but it isn't — you are hearing two complete conversations happening simultaneously, each coherent on its own, creating a third meaning in their combination. The tension between them is the drama.

This is the technical tool for your scenes where multiple characters interact. Not one after the other — both at once, the listener holding multiple narrative threads, the collision between them generating something neither theme alone could produce.

## Why This Work Is First

Everything in Module One teaches you something about narrative architecture. This work comes first because it does the most basic thing in the most transparent way: it shows you exactly how to make a character out of sound, and then it shows you what happens when one character destroys another and has to carry that fact forward in its body.

You will spend ninety minutes asking your listener to do what Prokofiev asks them to do in twenty-six minutes. Start here.
