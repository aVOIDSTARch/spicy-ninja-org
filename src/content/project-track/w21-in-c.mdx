*In C* is 53 short melodic fragments numbered 1 through 53. Each performer plays each fragment as many times as they choose before moving to the next. The only rule: no performer should be more than three or four fragments ahead of or behind the ensemble average. A single C pulse (C8 on piano, continuous eighth notes) underlies the entire performance.

No two performances of *In C* are identical. The piece is a mathematical system whose output is unpredictable from its rules. Individual musicians make micro-decisions that aggregate into macro-patterns nobody designed. The ensemble as a distributed intelligence.

## Your Orbital Mechanics in Simplest Form

Each character in *Jus Pulsus* has their own loop. Each loop has its own rhythm. No one controls the ensemble output. The emergent result is something no single loop contains and no composer explicitly wrote.

*In C* is this principle in its simplest possible form. The fact that it produces profoundly moving music is not an accident — it is a demonstration that musical meaning is an emergent property of structured interaction, not an injected quality.

## The Density Curve

As the ensemble moves through the 53 fragments, the aggregate texture shifts from sparse to dense to sparse again across approximately 45–90 minutes. The density curve is emergent — it arises from the statistical distribution of performers across the fragments, not from any instruction.

This is your Act I-to-III density arc. You do not need to write the density of every moment. Write the system. The arc follows from the mathematics. The question is not "how do I make this dense at the right moment" — the question is "what system produces the right density curve as an emergent property?"

## Write Rules, Not Notes

*In C* teaches the most important compositional shift available to you: from writing notes to writing rules that generate notes. Your live_loops are rules. The relationships between them are rules. The emergent music is what the rules produce when they interact. You do not control the output directly. You design the system. Then you listen to what the system says.
