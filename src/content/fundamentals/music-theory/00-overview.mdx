This branch teaches music theory as a compositional tool, not as academic taxonomy. Every concept has a direct application to decisions a composer needs to make. Nothing is here purely for completeness.

Four topics:

1. **Intervals** — the distances between notes, their names, their emotional weight, and how to work with them structurally
2. **Modes and ragas** — harmonic containers with rules for approach and departure; the Western modal system and its Indian raga equivalent
3. **The harmonic series** — where timbre comes from, why some intervals sound consonant and others dissonant, and what this means for instrumental casting
4. **Leitmotif construction** — what a leitmotif actually is psychologically, how to build one durable enough for a large-scale work, and how to develop it across acts

## Who This Branch Is For

This branch assumes you can hear the difference between notes but may not have formal vocabulary for what you're hearing. If you perceive pitch as graph-distance — spatial distance between points on a grid rather than as heard intervals — this branch works with that rather than against it. Interval arithmetic is exactly that: counting steps. The emotional character of intervals is worth knowing, but you don't need to retrain your perception. You need to connect your existing perceptual system to vocabulary the tradition has developed.

If you already have formal theory training, these lessons are calibrated toward application rather than completeness. Read them for the compositional framings rather than the basic definitions.

*Project connection: Theory knowledge is what converts compositional instinct into compositional control. The moments in a large programmatic work where a specific harmonic choice is needed — the right mode for a world music intrusion, the right interval for a character relationship, the reason a particular instrument feels inevitable in a role — all require the vocabulary this branch provides.*
