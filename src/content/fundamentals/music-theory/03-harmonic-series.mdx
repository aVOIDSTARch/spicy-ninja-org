Every pitched sound — a plucked string, a blown horn, a struck bell — is not a single frequency. It is a fundamental frequency plus a series of higher frequencies (overtones) occurring simultaneously, each at a specific mathematical ratio to the fundamental. This series is a physical fact, not a cultural construction. And it is the reason instruments have timbral personalities.

## The Series Itself

If the fundamental is C2 (65 Hz), the harmonic series ascending from it produces:

| Harmonic | Frequency ratio | Approximate pitch | Interval from C |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st (fundamental) | 1:1 | C2 | — |
| 2nd | 2:1 | C3 | Octave |
| 3rd | 3:2 | G3 | Perfect fifth |
| 4th | 4:3 | C4 | Perfect fourth |
| 5th | 5:4 | E4 | Major third |
| 6th | 6:5 | G4 | Minor third |
| 7th | 7:6 | B♭4 (slightly flat) | Minor seventh |
| 8th | 8:7 | C5 | Octave |

The first six harmonics produce the major chord. The major chord is not a cultural invention — it is the natural resonance pattern of vibrating matter. When you hear something resolve on a major chord, you are responding to a physical phenomenon older than music.

## Why This Matters for Timbre

Instruments differ not in which harmonics are present but in *which harmonics are loud*. A clarinet emphasises odd-numbered harmonics and suppresses even ones — which is why it sounds hollow and pure. A French horn has strong upper harmonics that give it authority and carrying power. A viola emphasises the lower-middle harmonics, producing warmth and a slight introspective quality. An oboe has a nasal, slightly reedy character because of a specific emphasis in the mid-upper harmonics.

**This means instrumental casting is not aesthetic preference — it is physics.** An instrument's role in a programmatic work should be determined in part by what its harmonic profile communicates, not just by what register it occupies or what tradition it belongs to.

A French horn's authority is not a cultural association built up over centuries of horn-playing in orchestras. It is a consequence of a harmonic profile that projects dominance in acoustic space — the upper harmonics carry further and fill a room in a way that is physically unavoidable. A viola's warmth is the acoustic result of emphasized lower-middle harmonics. A double bass produces fundamental frequencies in the range where pitch perception begins to dissolve into tactile sensation — the note is felt before it is heard.

When casting is right — when the instrument's physical harmonic profile matches the character's narrative personality — the casting feels not chosen but discovered.

## Consonance and Dissonance as Physics

Intervals that appear early in the harmonic series sound consonant because they occur naturally together in physical resonance. The octave (2:1) and perfect fifth (3:2) are the most consonant intervals because they share the most harmonic frequencies.

The minor second (approximately 16:15) is deeply dissonant because the ratio is complex and the two frequencies create interference patterns — the waveforms fight each other rather than reinforcing. This is not a cultural rule. The physics of wave superposition produces the perception of roughness and tension between closely spaced frequencies.

When you use a specific dissonant interval deliberately as a tension device — a half-step pressure between two simultaneous voices — you are working with wave interference physics. The listener's nervous system registers the interference before the conscious mind processes the harmonic content.

## The Harmonic Series in Sonic Pi

```ruby
# Build a chord from the harmonic series above C2
# This is what the C2 fundamental "actually sounds like" — its harmonic content made visible
fundamental = :c2
harmonic_series = [fundamental, :c3, :g3, :c4, :e4, :g4]

# Amplitude decreases with harmonic number — approximating natural physics
live_loop :harmonic_demonstration do
  harmonic_series.each_with_index do |h, i|
    play h,
      amp:     1.0 / (i + 1),   # each harmonic quieter than the last
      sustain: 4,
      release: 1
  end
  sleep 6
end
```

Run this and listen. You are hearing the physics of a single low C — its full harmonic content spread out and made individually audible. A real instrument playing C2 produces exactly this, with the harmonics blended into a single perceived timbre.

## Beating and Sustained Tension

When two tones near each other in frequency are held simultaneously, they produce beating — a slow amplitude oscillation whose frequency equals the difference between the two tones. At 440 Hz and 444 Hz, the beating is 4 Hz — four oscillations per second, clearly audible.

La Monte Young's drone works (see Project Track W14) use this phenomenon deliberately: sustained tones from the harmonic series, held long enough for the interference patterns between them to become the music. The beating IS the music. It changes in character as overtone relationships shift.

In practical composition, beating creates a specific kind of sustained physical tension between two voices — not melodic tension, not rhythmic tension, but acoustic tension in the body. Two sustained voices a minor second apart beat against each other at a rate the nervous system registers as urgency.

```ruby
# Two voices a minor second apart — the beating is audible and physical
live_loop :tension_demonstration do
  play :e4, sustain: 8, amp: 0.5
  play :f4, sustain: 8, amp: 0.5
  sleep 8
end
```

*Project connection: The harmonic series explains why certain instrumental castings in programmatic work feel physically inevitable rather than chosen. When an instrument's harmonic profile matches a character's emotional register — dominant, searching, grounding, ancient — the casting has physics behind it. The instrument doesn't represent the character; it physically produces the same qualities.*
