The interview is the last stage of character development. By the time you reach it, the character has a sonic identity, a narrative function, relational structure, and a development arc. The interview's purpose is not to establish those things. It is to discover what you don't yet know about the character — the details that will make them feel inhabited rather than constructed.

The interview questions cannot be answered analytically. They require you to know the character well enough to answer on their behalf. If you cannot answer a question, that is information: the character is not yet finished.

Resistance is compositional material. If a question produces discomfort, uncertainty, or a feeling that you are making something up rather than discovering something, that is the signal to work that question harder. What emerges from the difficult answer is often the most important thing about the character.

## The Ten Questions

**1. What does this character smell like?**

Not sound like. Not look like. Smell. Smell is the oldest sense, the one most directly connected to memory and the limbic system. The answer to this question often produces the character's emotional register more precisely than any technical description. The answer should be specific enough that someone who reads it can smell it.

**2. What does this character want more than anything?**

In the context of the work, not in general. What do they want from the other characters? From the events of the piece? From the universe they are in? The answer defines their dramatic function — what they move toward, what they refuse to give up, what the central event costs them specifically.

**3. What are they afraid of?**

Not their antagonist, not their dramatic obstacle. The thing underneath those — the fear that the obstacle activates. What does this character need to not happen? The answer often reveals a vulnerability that can be encoded in their leitmotif: a note they approach cautiously, a register they avoid, a rhythm that destabilizes them.

**4. What do they sound like in a moment of pure joy?**

Not their normal state. Pure joy — unmixed, unqualified. Most composers never hear their characters in this state because it doesn't appear in the work. But knowing it changes how you write every other state.

**5. What do they sound like in grief?**

Grief, not distress. Not drama. Grief is quiet, sustained, particular. It has a specific quality different from sadness, from loss, from anger. A character's grief state is often the key to their aftermath material — how they sound after the structural event.

**6. Do they know they are a character in a constructed work, or do they experience themselves as real?**

This question has no wrong answer. But the answer changes how the character behaves. A character who knows they are in a myth behaves differently from one who experiences everything as immediate reality. The answer is often different from what you initially assume.

**7. What is their relationship to silence?**

Do they seek it or avoid it? Do they fill it instinctively or rest in it? Silence is a compositional element and each character uses it differently. The character's relationship to silence is their relationship to rest, to uncertainty, to the unspoken.

**8. If the central crisis of the work happens to them directly: what do they sound like in the moment of transformation?**

Not before. Not after. The moment itself — the instant of change. This is the hardest question to answer, and the most important one compositionally. The answer must be specific enough to be scored.

**9. If the central crisis happens to someone else: what do they do?**

Do they move toward it or away? Do they witness in silence or comment? Do they try to intervene? The answer reveals the character's ethical structure — not their stated values but their revealed behavior under pressure.

**10. What is the most surprising thing about this character that you didn't know when you started?**

This question is asked last because it cannot be answered first. It requires the other nine to have been worked through. The answer is often the thing that makes the character feel real rather than designed. Write it down. It frequently generates the best compositional material in the entire process.

## Using the Answers

The answers to these questions are not biographical material to be stored. They are compositional material to be translated.

The smell answer gives you the emotional register: encode it as a dynamic range, a harmonic color, a spatial position.

The fear answer gives you a vulnerability: encode it as an interval the character avoids, a rhythm that destabilizes them, a register they never inhabit comfortably.

The joy answer gives you a state for Act I or Act IV: what the character sounds like before they have been changed, or what they move toward as transformation resolves.

The grief answer gives you the aftermath: what they sound like after the structural event, specifically.

The surprise answer gives you the unexpected detail that makes the character feel inhabited — the thing that was discovered rather than designed.
