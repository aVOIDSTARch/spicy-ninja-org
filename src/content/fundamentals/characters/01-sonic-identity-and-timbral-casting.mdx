The most consequential decision in casting a programmatic character is not what notes they play. It is what they sound like. Timbre — the specific quality of a sound — is character identity at its most fundamental. The character does not play their theme on their instrument. They *are* their instrument.

## The Prokofiev Standard

Prokofiev's casting in *Peter and the Wolf* is the clearest demonstration of this principle:

The **wolf** is three French horns in unison. Not because Prokofiev needed a loud instrument. Because the horn in its lower register has a specific, irreducible character: heavy, implacable, slightly threatening even at medium volume. The overtone series of a horn is naturally dominant — it fills a room the way a predator fills a doorway. This is not metaphor. It is acoustics.

The **duck** is an oboe. Reedy, slightly nasal, slightly comic. Not because ducks are funny, but because an oboe in its middle register has exactly that quality — earnest, slightly ungainly, endearing in spite of itself. When the duck is swallowed by the wolf, her theme persists inside the wolf's theme. The oboe color survives inside the horn color, transformed. That survival would be impossible if the casting had been arbitrary.

The lesson: when the casting is right, narrative events between characters produce musical events that are *inevitable* rather than constructed.

## Timbral Identity Checklist

Before a sound can be a character, it needs:

**A register that is exclusively theirs.** Two characters in the same register will blur and compete. The sub-bass belongs to one character. The high shimmer belongs to another. This is not a stylistic preference — it is structural. If two characters share register, the listener cannot track them simultaneously.

**A dynamic behavior that reflects personality.** A character who surges and recedes is different from one who maintains a consistent level. A character who is always quiet is different from one who is sometimes quiet and sometimes enormous. Dynamic behavior is behavior.

**A rhythmic signature at rest.** Every character, when they are simply present and not doing anything narrative, has a characteristic way of moving through time. The Ground breathes slowly. A trickster character might have an irregular, unpredictable pulse. A grief character might have long sustained tones with abrupt silences. This signature is the character thinking without speaking.

**An interval vocabulary.** Which intervals does this character favor? Which do they avoid? This becomes the character's harmonic personality — the set of distances they naturally create between themselves and other characters.

## Register Architecture

In a multi-character electronic work, register architecture functions like stage blocking: where each character lives in the frequency spectrum determines what relationships are visible, what conflicts can occur, and what harmonies are possible.

A useful starting framework:

| Zone | Range | Character quality |
|---|---|---|
| Sub-bass | 20–80Hz | Substrate, permanence, ancient forces |
| Bass | 80–300Hz | Grounding, harmonic root, slow narratives |
| Low-mid | 300–800Hz | Human vocal register, searching, vulnerable |
| Mid | 800Hz–3kHz | Presence, action, primary melodic zone |
| High-mid | 3–8kHz | Commentary, precision, the analytical mind |
| Air | 8kHz+ | Shimmer, transcendence, the very distant |

Assigning each character a primary zone, with limited excursions into adjacent zones, maintains sonic separation across the full complexity of a large cast.

## Timbre in Electronic Music

In acoustic programmatic music, casting is constrained by available instruments. In electronic music, the palette is infinite — which makes the decision harder, not easier. The question is not "what is available" but "what is exactly right."

The discipline: for each character, identify the one acoustic analog that comes closest to the character's personality, then use that as a reference while building or choosing an electronic sound. The character does not have to use that acoustic sound — but the acoustic reference tells you what you are aiming for.

*Project connection: The work currently in development uses this method explicitly. Each character has an acoustic casting reference and an electronic implementation target. The casting process is documented as a compositional decision, not a sound design decision, because the distinction matters: sound design serves the composition. Casting is the composition.*
