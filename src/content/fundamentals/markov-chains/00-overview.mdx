A Markov chain is a system where the next state depends only on the current state, according to a set of probabilities. In music: the next note depends on the current note, and how likely each possible next note is.

This is not randomness. It is *structured randomness* — chaos with rules. The output is unpredictable in detail but predictable in character.

## Why This Branch Exists

Several things in *Jus Pulsus* need to feel improvised without being arbitrary:

- The Bell's sparse appearances in Act I — accidental-seeming but following an internal logic
- The African Drumming's response to other characters — reactive, alive, never mechanical
- The Three Fates' commentary — they seem to be listening and responding, not reading a script
- The Protagonist's searching melodic behavior — following a thought, not a pattern

Markov chains give you the tool to make something sound alive without having to improvise in real time or compose every note explicitly.

## Three Lessons

1. **First-order chains**: Each note chooses the next based on a probability table
2. **Weighted random in Sonic Pi**: `choose`, `wrand`, and `ring` as practical tools
3. **Higher-order chains and memory**: When the next note depends on the last two — more sophisticated behavior, longer-range coherence

## The Deeper Principle

Composing a Markov chain for a character is an act of character development. The Bell does not know what note it will play next — but it has tendencies. It is more likely to stay in the pentatonic range. It is less likely to leap a major seventh. Those tendencies are its personality encoded as probability.
