Bach Math is not music history. It is a toolkit.

The six lessons in this branch teach you to perform mathematical operations on musical material — the same operations Bach used to generate 14 complete fugues and 4 canons from a single subject. Every operation translates directly to Sonic Pi code. When you understand them, you can derive infinite variation from any single theme through transformation rather than invention.

## What Mathematical Operations on Music Means

A musical theme is a sequence of pitches and durations. Like any sequence, it can be reversed, reflected, scaled, and combined with overlapping copies of itself. These are not metaphors — they are literal mathematical operations on arrays of data.

**Retrograde** plays the theme backwards. **Inversion** flips every interval upside down. **Retrograde inversion** does both. **Augmentation** doubles every duration. **Diminution** halves every duration. **Stretto** overlaps the theme with itself before the first statement has finished.

Each operation produces a recognisably related variant. The retrograde sounds like the same identity moving in the opposite direction through time. The inversion sounds like the same character seen from the other side of a relationship. Augmentation sounds like the character inhabiting a different, slower timescale. They are not different themes — they are the same theme in different states.

## Why This Matters for Programmatic Music

In any programmatic work where characters have leitmotifs that develop across acts, Bach Math is the primary toolkit for motivated transformation. A theme that begins as one thing and arrives as something different has almost always passed through some subset of these operations. The transformation feels motivated — caused by events — precisely because the mathematical relationship to the original is preserved even as the character of the result changes.

Bach discovered this systematically. The *Art of Fugue* (1740–50) is the most extreme demonstration: 14 complete fugues and 4 canons derived from a single subject through these operations, combined simultaneously in every configuration the mathematics permits. The last fugue breaks off unfinished. His son added a note to the manuscript: "The author died here."

## The Sonic Pi Connection

Every Bach operation has a direct Sonic Pi equivalent using array transformations. `.reverse` for retrograde. Interval arithmetic with `.map` for inversion. Duration scaling with `.map` for augmentation and diminution. Offset `live_loop` timing for stretto. This branch teaches both the compositional concept and its Sonic Pi implementation simultaneously.

You are not learning music theory for its own sake. You are learning compositional tools that run in your actual environment.

*Project connection: In any programmatic work with a large cast, leitmotif transformation is the difference between themes that feel decoratively varied and themes that feel like characters who have been through something. This branch is the mechanical foundation for that.*
