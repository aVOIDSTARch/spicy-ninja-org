Bach Math is not music history. It is a toolkit.

The six lessons in this branch teach you to perform mathematical operations on musical material — the same operations Bach used to generate 14 complete fugues and 4 canons from a single subject. These operations translate directly to Sonic Pi code. When you understand them, you can derive infinite variation from any single leitmotif through transformation rather than invention.

## Why This Branch Exists

Jus Pulsus is built on a structural insight: each character has a leitmotif, and those leitmotifs develop by transformation across the four acts. The Cosmos' string theme in Act IV is not a new theme — it is the Act I theme that has passed through the kintsugi wound. Pulsus' brass pattern in the ecstatic tempest is not chaos — it is the opening pattern augmented, inverted, pressed into extreme density.

Bach figured out how to do this systematically. You are going to learn his method and apply it to electronic music.

## What You Will Be Able to Do

After completing this branch, you can take any leitmotif and produce:

- Its retrograde (backwards)
- Its inversion (upside down)
- Its retrograde inversion (both)
- Its augmentation (half speed)
- Its diminution (double speed)
- A stretto version (overlapping voices entering before the previous completes)

And you will be able to combine these transformations to create the sense that a character has been changed by events — without abandoning the identity that makes them recognizable.

## The Sonic Pi Connection

Every Bach operation has a direct Sonic Pi equivalent. This branch teaches both simultaneously. You are not learning music theory for its own sake. You are learning compositional tools that run in your actual environment.
