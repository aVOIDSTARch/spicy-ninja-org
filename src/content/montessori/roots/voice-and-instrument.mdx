Every instrument has a voice. Not a range, not a set of technical capabilities — a *voice*, with all that implies: personality, register, emotional bandwidth, characteristic responses to pressure. A French horn played softly in its low register sounds nothing like a French horn played at full capacity in its upper register. They are almost different instruments. A composer who doesn't know this is writing blind.

This root is about the relationship between timbre and identity — the question of what makes a sonic character recognisable, consistent, and capable of dramatic development.

## The casting problem

*Jus Pulsus* is scored for specific ensembles specifically because of their voices: brass-wind for Pulsus (embodied, searching, capable of sustained grief), string quartet for the Cosmos/Eurydice (layered awakening from geological bass to bright violin), percussion for the African Drumming character (the oldest collective technology in human music).

This is not orchestration as decoration. It is dramatic casting. The instrument *is* the character.

From here the tree opens toward **Indian Classical** — where the relationship between raga, performer, and instrument creates something unrepeatable — and toward the **Björk Bodysuit** bespoke journey, where a single artist's use of voice as primary compositional material redefines what an instrument can be.
