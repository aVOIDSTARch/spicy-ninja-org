Sufi devotional music — particularly the qawwali tradition and the sema ceremony of the Mevlevi order — uses musical structure not for aesthetic pleasure but for spiritual technology. The repetition, the gradual intensification, the call and response, the breath-controlled spinning — all precisely calculated to produce altered states in performers and listeners.

This is not incidental. The mathematics of these forms — the cycle lengths, the rhythmic densities, the maqam modes and their emotional associations — are part of a centuries-long empirical tradition of studying what music does to the body and mind.

## The connection to the project

The Cosmos character in *Jus Pulsus* draws on this tradition — particularly the image of the whirling dervish as someone who has found stillness through motion. The Cosmos before Pulsus arrives is not static. It is in perpetual self-contained motion that looks like stillness from outside.

This branch is underdeveloped in the current tree and will expand as the project's world music research phase deepens.
