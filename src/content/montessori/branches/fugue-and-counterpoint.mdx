A fugue is not a form in the way a sonata is a form. It is a *texture* and a set of procedures. A single idea — the subject — is subjected to systematic transformation across multiple voices, each independent, each tracking its own path, the whole forming a coherent argument that none of the individual voices could make alone.

This is the structural model for *Jus Pulsus*. Not literally — the work is not a fugue — but architecturally. Multiple independent musical characters each have their own leitmotif, their own timbral identity, their own developmental arc. They combine, separate, transform each other through contact, and generate emergent complexity that no character produces alone.

## The four transformations

Bach's four transformations — Original (P), Inversion (I), Retrograde (R), Retrograde Inversion (RI) — form a mathematical group under composition. Understanding these transformations is foundational to understanding how a leitmotif can be wounded, scarred, and rebuilt while remaining recognisable. The kintsugi mechanism in *Jus Pulsus* depends on this.
