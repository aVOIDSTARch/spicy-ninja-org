Electronic music is not a genre. It is a technological condition — music made possible by the existence of electricity, and transformed repeatedly by each successive wave of electronic technology. The theremin, the Ondes Martenot, the tape machine, the Moog, the TR-808, the sampler, the DAW — each created new musical possibilities and new communities of practice around those possibilities.

The lineage that matters for this project runs through a specific channel: the moment in the late 1980s and early 1990s when electronic music stopped being the domain of academic composers in studios and became dance music. When the machine rhythm became the body rhythm. When synthesis became a democratic tool.

## The foundational experience

Paul van Dyk's *Transport* (1994) is the personal entry point — not because it is historically canonical but because it was the first piece of music that demonstrated to the composer of this project that electronic music could be simultaneously physically overwhelming and emotionally precise. That the machine could be as expressive as any acoustic instrument.

This is the lineage that *Jus Pulsus* is made from — even as it reaches back to African drumming and Indian classical and Bach for its structural logic.
