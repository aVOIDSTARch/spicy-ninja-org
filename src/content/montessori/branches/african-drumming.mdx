Western music thinks of rhythm as a single line underneath melody — the drummer keeps time so the melody can float above. West African drumming inverts this. There is no underneath. Every rhythmic voice is in the foreground. Multiple complete rhythmic patterns play simultaneously, each in a different metric feel, interlocking in ways that create emergent patterns none of the individual voices contains.

This is polyrhythm not as complexity for its own sake but as a different theory of musical democracy: every voice contributes equally to the whole, and the whole cannot be heard from any single voice's perspective.

## The Olatunji connection

Babatunde Olatunji's *Drums of Passion* (1959) is on the Project Track syllabus specifically because it was the first time this music was heard by a mass Western audience — and because it directly influenced the generation of composers (including Steve Reich) who would develop minimalism from its principles. Reich went to Ghana to study drumming, came back, and wrote *Drumming*.

## The superpartner

In *Jus Pulsus*, the African Drumming character's superpartner is The Chant — two halves of the same prehistoric musical technology. Rhythm and melody before their separation into different instruments and different roles.
